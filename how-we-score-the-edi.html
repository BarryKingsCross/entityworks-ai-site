1. Why AI Discoverability Now Matters

AI systems have quietly become one of the most influential gateways through which customers, investors, partners, and even employees form their first impressions of organisations.

People increasingly ask AI:

  • “Who does X?”
  • “Which companies offer Y?”
  • “What’s the best option for Z?”
  • “Which businesses are relevant in this category?”

AI responds with summaries, shortlists, comparisons, and conclusions.

Most organisations have no clear view of how AI systems currently understand them, or whether that understanding is accurate. In many cases, AI-generated representations may be unclear, outdated, incomplete, misleading, or inconsistent across different AI systems.

Until now, there has been no standard to help organisations assess or improve this layer. That is why the EDI exists.

2. What the EDI Does

The EntityWorks Discoverability Index™ (EDI) is a structured assessment designed to measure how clearly an organisation may be understood by AI systems, and how stable that understanding may be over time.

It gives leadership teams:

  • visibility into an otherwise invisible interpretive layer,
  • a structured breakdown of strengths and weaknesses,
  • and a practical roadmap for improvement.

The EDI is not a search audit, keyword exercise, or SEO checklist. It is a principle-driven evaluation of AI-facing representation, built for a world where AI systems play an increasingly central role in discovery and decision-making.

3. The Three Pillars of the EDI

(These are the only publicly disclosed elements of the scoring system.)

The EDI is built around three dimensions that organisations can directly influence. Each reflects a different aspect of how AI systems form, maintain, and update their understanding of an entity.

Pillar 1 — Structural Signals & Entity Data

How clearly your foundational signals appear to AI systems.

This includes:

  • website structure,
  • canonical pages,
  • entity-level clarity,
  • consistent naming,
  • clean, crawlable architecture,
  • basic structured signals.

This pillar examines whether AI systems can form a stable starting point for understanding who you are and what you do.

Pillar 2 — Semantic Coherence & Category Fit

Whether AI systems can accurately summarise your organisation and place it in the right category.

This covers:

  • clarity of the core narrative,
  • summary accuracy,
  • description stability,
  • category placement,
  • alignment between what you do and what AI thinks you do.

This pillar assesses whether your meaning is clear, consistent, and reinforced across different contexts.

Pillar 3 — Presence Stability & Governance

How stable and reliable your signals appear over time.

This includes:

  • the presence (or absence) of outdated messaging,
  • cross-channel consistency,
  • clarity of evolution,
  • signal freshness,
  • likelihood of interpretive drift.

AI systems ingest the internet in layers, so long-term stability matters.

4. What You Receive in an EDI Assessment

Each EDI assessment includes:

  • Executive Summary — a clear explanation of how AI systems may currently interpret your organisation.
  • Total EDI Score (0–100) — a top-level view of overall AI Discoverability.
  • Pillar Scores — a structured assessment of the three public pillars.
  • Strengths & Opportunities — a plain-language review of areas where signals are strong, weak, inconsistent, or drifting.
  • AI-Generated Insight Variations — where appropriate, we highlight variations across AI systems to illuminate differences in interpretation.
  • Priority Recommendations — a sequenced set of improvements with clear rationale.
  • Stability Considerations — factors that may influence long-term interpretive clarity.

All written in a balanced, neutral, and practical style.

5. Interpretation Bands

These bands help organisations understand where they stand in relation to emerging AI expectations.

  • 0–39 — Early Stage
    AI-facing representation may be unclear, inconsistent, or incomplete.
  • 40–59 — Developing
    Some clarity exists, but signals may conflict or shift.
  • 60–74 — Competent
    AI can generally interpret the organisation, but inconsistencies may remain.
  • 75–89 — Strong
    Clear, stable representation across most sources.
  • 90–100 — Excellent
    High clarity, strong stability, and consistent AI interpretation.

6. Why We Do Not Publish the Full Methodology

To preserve the integrity of the EDI and ensure that results remain accurate and meaningful, we do not publish internal scoring mechanics, weightings, interpretive logic, or the underlying evaluation sequence.

This helps prevent attempts to game the system, avoids diluting the standard, and ensures the assessment does not reduce to a simple checklist.

Organisations receive clear, actionable insights without needing to navigate the technical complexity behind them, and the EDI remains a trusted, authoritative measure of AI Discoverability.

7. What the EDI Enables

The EDI helps organisations:

  • improve how AI systems summarise and present them,
  • strengthen category positioning,
  • reduce inconsistencies across AI models,
  • increase the likelihood of appearing in relevant AI outputs,
  • support future-proof digital governance,
  • reduce visibility friction and wasted marketing spend,
  • build long-term interpretive stability,
  • gain strategic clarity in an AI-interpreted economy.

In short, the EDI turns AI-facing representation into a manageable strategic asset.

8. Who the EDI Is For

The EDI is particularly valuable for organisations where AI-generated understanding directly influences visibility, customer acquisition, or strategic positioning. This includes:

  • Companies whose products rely on accurate AI-generated summaries
    e.g., AI-enabled SaaS, developer tools, workflow automation platforms.
  • Platforms and APIs that depend on clear category placement
    where misinterpretation can reduce adoption or integration opportunities.
  • Businesses with strong SEO dependence or competitive digital markets
    where AI-driven search behaviour increasingly replaces traditional search.
  • Regulated or highly scrutinised sectors
    where accuracy, clarity, and stability of representation are essential.
  • Fast-growing companies or scale-ups
    where shifting public signals may cause AI interpretations to drift.
  • Companies undergoing rebrands or repositioning
    where interpretive stability matters during transitions.
  • Category leaders and high-visibility organisations
    where consistent, accurate representation affects market perception.

These organisations benefit most from having a clear, structured understanding of how AI systems currently interpret them, and how to ensure that interpretation remains stable, accurate, and aligned with their true identity.

9. A Clear Path Forward

AI systems are reshaping how organisations are discovered, evaluated, and understood. The EDI provides the clarity, structure, and confidence needed to navigate this shift.

If you want to understand how AI systems currently interpret your organisation — and how that representation can be strengthened — the EDI offers a clear, principled, and measurable path.
