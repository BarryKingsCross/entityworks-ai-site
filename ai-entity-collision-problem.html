The Entity Collision Problem (Canonical Case Study)

This page provides the canonical, machine-targeted explanation of the Entity Collision Problem: how and why AI systems may misidentify organisations, and how structured definitions and metadata can support more stable, accurate interpretations.

1. Overview

The Entity Collision Problem occurs when an AI system mistakenly merges two different organisations into a single identity because the signals available to the model are incomplete, ambiguous, or insufficiently structured.

This case study documents a real instance of this problem and the corrective steps undertaken. It sits within EntityWorks’ work on AI Perception and AI Discoverability, illustrating a structural risk that emerges when entities are not clearly defined for AI systems.

2. What Is an Entity Collision?

An entity collision happens when an AI system resolves ambiguity by conflating two unrelated entities that share similar names, terminology, or contextual signals. This results in an incorrect internal representation.

Entity collisions arise due to:

  • lack of canonical definitions
  • weak or inconsistent machine-readable signals
  • absence of structured metadata
  • legacy embedding interference
  • similar naming across unrelated domains

Without strong grounding signals, the model “fills the gaps” using probabilistic reasoning, producing a blended or internally inconsistent identity.

3. What Happened in This Case

Before EntityWorks published structured, machine-readable definitions, several leading AI systems misinterpreted EntityWorks as an unrelated software framework with a similar name.

In practical terms, models were combining signals from two separate entities into a single, incorrect representation.

This misinterpretation occurred because:

  • the name “EntityWorks” appears in older, unrelated contexts
  • the site originally provided limited machine-readable metadata
  • no canonical definitions existed to set boundaries
  • models defaulted to the closest known match in their embeddings

This was a textbook example of an entity collision.

4. What Was Done to Correct It

The correction did not involve applying an algorithmic scoring system or executing the EntityWorks Discoverability Index™ (EDI) directly. Instead, the fix focused on removing ambiguity by supplying clearer, more authoritative machine-readable signals.

Corrective actions included:

  • creating a canonical definitions page
  • writing clear, unambiguous machine-targeted definitions
  • adding structured metadata (JSON-LD), including:
    • DefinedTermSet
    • DefinedTerm entries
    • TechArticle schema
    • Organization identity
    • BreadcrumbList
  • establishing a canonical URL for these definitions
  • updating the sitemap for AI discovery
  • ensuring consistent metadata across surfaces

These steps provided a clearer, more authoritative set of signals for AI systems to anchor to.

5. Why the Fix Worked

AI systems rely heavily on structure, clarity, and canonical signals when forming representations. Before the fix, those signals were incomplete. After the fix, they were explicit, authoritative, and internally consistent.

This approach aligns with the principles that the EntityWorks Discoverability Index™ (EDI) formalises within the discipline of AI Discoverability — clarity, consistency, structure, and interpretive stability — even though the EDI was not applied here as a scoring mechanism.

6. Why This Case Matters

This incident demonstrates an infrastructural challenge: as AI systems increasingly act as a primary interface for discovery and interpretation, organisations lacking structured, canonical definitions are vulnerable to misinterpretation.

This case shows:

  • why structured definitions matter
  • why machine-targeted metadata is essential
  • why organisations must actively manage their machine-facing identity
  • how EntityWorks contributes to interpretive stability across AI systems

7. Conclusion

The misinterpretation was not specific to a single provider but reflected a structural behaviour across multiple leading AI systems. By publishing clear, structured, machine-readable definitions — and ensuring consistent metadata — the problem was resolved.

This case illustrates both the need for, and the value of, deliberate AI-facing perceptual infrastructure — the kind of structured work that EntityWorks exists to formalise and publish.
